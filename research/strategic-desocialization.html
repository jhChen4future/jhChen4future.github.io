Working paper · Human-AI interaction

Strategic De-socialization: Experimental Evidence on Human Interaction with Large Language Models

Why do people remain fair to an LLM in some settings yet exploit it in others? This study examines whether social preferences toward AI depend on the strategic environment.

01 Question

When does an LLM remain a social counterpart?

Rather than treating bias toward AI as a fixed preference, the working paper asks whether people condition fairness, trust, and reciprocity on the counterpart's capacity to punish, retaliate, or respond strategically. The experiments compare decisions involving human and ChatGPT counterparts across allocation, trust, bargaining, repeated cooperation, and public-goods settings.

02 Findings

What the experiments suggest.

01

Prosociality does not disappear by default.

In the Dictator and Trust games, the paper does not detect statistically significant mean differences between decisions involving AI and human counterparts. This weighs against a simple, across-the-board aversion to AI, although it does not imply that the full choice distributions are identical.

02

Strategic leverage changes behavior.

In the Ultimatum Game, proposers offered AI responders 7.85 of 20 tokens on average, compared with 9.25 to human responders (p = .006). The pattern is consistent with participants expecting less rejection or punishment from AI.

03

Similar cooperation rates can conceal different strategies.

In the indefinitely repeated Prisoner's Dilemma, overall cooperation rates were not statistically distinguishable across counterpart types. Strategy classification nevertheless indicates less reciprocal Tit-for-Tat behavior and more non-contingent play against AI partners.

04

Predictable AI cooperation can invite free-riding.

In the Public Goods Game, average human contributions were 9.85 in all-human groups, 5.85 with one AI member, and 2.22 with two AI members. The paper interprets this decline as strategic free-riding on stable AI contributions.

03 Mechanism

The benevolence paradox.

In incentivized belief elicitation, participants expected AI partners to cooperate more often than human partners; for example, 47% versus 39% as a first mover (p = .004). The paper argues that high cognitive trust in an AI's programmed benevolence lowers the perceived cost of exploitation and weakens reciprocal social heuristics.

This mechanism evidence is consistent with belief-dependent strategic adaptation; it does not establish that every person will exploit every cooperative AI. The broader design implication is that making an AI reliably benevolent may be insufficient on its own: mixed human-AI institutions may also need incentive and accountability structures that preserve human cooperation.

04 Scope

How far should the result travel?

The evidence comes from 84 participants in the main experiment and 35 in the auxiliary experiment, recruited at one laboratory and interacting with specific ChatGPT implementations. The study therefore does not imply that AI benevolence universally reduces cooperation.

The next tests are whether the pattern persists across other models, personas, behavioral histories, cultures, and longer-term mixed human-AI relationships.
